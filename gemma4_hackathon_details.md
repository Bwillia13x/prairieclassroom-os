Description
Every challenge has a perfect match, and the clock is ticking. Real innovation happens when we build for the places that need it most. This might be a classroom with spotty internet, a medical site far from a data center, or a community where privacy is non-negotiable.

With the release of Gemma 4, a new family of open models is officially in your hands. Leverage local frontier intelligence, native function calling, and multimodal understanding to tackle the issues that affect your community.

We challenge you to use this groundbreaking technology to build solutions that create meaningful, positive change in the world. This is your opportunity to drive impact in areas like:

Health & Sciences: Bridge the gap between humans and data. Build tools that accelerate discovery or democratize knowledge.
Global Resilience: Build the systems of tomorrow—from offline, edge-based disaster response to long-range climate mitigation—that anticipate, mitigate, and respond to the world’s most pressing challenges.
Future of Education: Reimagine the learning journey by building multi-tool agents that adapt to the individual and empower the educator through seamless integration.
Digital Equity & Inclusivity: Break down barriers through linguistic diversity, intuitive interfaces, and tools that help close the AI skills gap.
Safety & Trust: Pioneer frameworks for transparency and reliability, ensuring AI remains grounded and explainable.

Whether you’re optimizing E2B and E4B models for edge-based solutions or deploying the 26B and 31B weights for complex tasks, every contribution pushes the boundaries of what AI can achieve.

  SCORING BREAKDOWN (Judge's Perspective)

  Impact & Vision — 33/40

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  This is where PrairieClassroom OS excels most. The judges are looking for "clearly and compellingly addresses a significant
  real-world problem" with "tangible potential for positive change." The 40-point weight means vision matters more than code.
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Strengths:
  - The problem framing is exceptional. "Classroom complexity is a coordination problem, not a content problem" is a thesis
  that immediately separates this from every generic ed-tech chatbot. Judges will have seen dozens of "AI tutor" submissions —
   this one reframes the entire category.
  - The Mrs. Okafor persona is specific, believable, and emotionally grounding. The six students (Amira, Brody, Chantal,
  Daniyal, Elena, Farid) each represent real constraints that Alberta teachers face daily.
  - The closed feedback loop (interventions → patterns → plans → briefings) is the architectural signature. It shows
  compounding value — the system gets better as teachers use it. This is the kind of insight that wins competitions.
  - Safety-first design (observational language, 15 forbidden terms, mandatory approval gates) shows mature thinking about
  deploying AI in professional settings with minors.
  - Privacy-first / local-first architecture directly addresses the hackathon's emphasis on "communities where privacy is
  non-negotiable."

  Gaps that cost points:
  - No evidence of real teacher validation. The writeup describes the system in professional detail but never says "we tested
  this with teachers" or "we observed this in a real classroom." A quote from even one teacher would be worth more than 10
  technical paragraphs.
  - Impact is theoretical. There's no data on time savings, coordination improvement, or teacher workflow reduction. Even
  estimated metrics ("differentiating a worksheet goes from 30 minutes to 2 seconds") would strengthen the case.
  - The "inclusive classrooms" framing could go further. The hackathon specifically mentions "Digital Equity & Inclusivity" —
  the multilingual vocab cards and EAL support are assets, but the writeup doesn't explicitly frame them as equity tools.
  - Scale story is thin. How many classrooms in Alberta look like this? What's the addressable impact? The hackathon wants
  "tangible potential" — quantify it.

  Video Pitch & Storytelling — 0/30

  This is the most critical gap. The evaluation criteria explicitly states: "Your project will be judged primarily on your
  video demo."

  There is no video. The demo script (docs/demo-script.md) is excellent — 15 minutes, well-structured, narration cues
  included. But a demo script is not a video.

  What winning videos from past Gemma competitions look like:
  - 2-3 minutes (hard limit)
  - Open with the problem (30 seconds of emotional hook)
  - Show the product working live (90 seconds of demo)
  - Close with impact and technical credibility (30 seconds)
  - High production quality — screen recording + voiceover at minimum, ideally with a face on camera

  What you need to produce:
  - Condense the 15-minute demo script to 3 minutes
  - Record the system running with real Gemma 4 inference (not mock)
  - Narrate the Mrs. Okafor story as the through-line
  - Show the closed loop in action: log → pattern → plan → briefing
  - End on the safety story and the "this runs locally" point

  Technical Depth & Execution — 22/30

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  The 30 points for technical depth are "verified by the code repository and writeup." Judges will scan the repo for: (1) real
   Gemma usage, not wrapper code, (2) structured architecture with clear engineering decisions, (3) working code that matches
  demo claims. The emphasis on "innovative use of Gemma 4's unique features" means Gemma-specific integration matters.
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Strengths (what earns high marks):
  - Architecture is production-grade. Three-service design, versioned prompt contracts, Zod validation, SQLite per-classroom
  memory, dual-tier routing — this is not a weekend prototype. The 32 ADRs in the decision log show engineering maturity.
  - Eval framework is exceptional. 64 golden-case evals across 5 categories (schema, content, safety, latency, synthesis) with
   zero regressions across 13 sprints. Few hackathon submissions will have anything comparable.
  - Dual-tier routing is a genuine Gemma-specific design. Using the small model for live tasks and large model with thinking
  for planning is architecturally sound and demonstrates understanding of the model family's trade-offs.
  - Safety enforcement is embedded, not bolted on. Prompt contracts, forbidden term lists, observational language rules, and
  approval gates show a level of safety thinking that judges from Google will appreciate.
  - Codebase is real. ~15,500 lines of TypeScript + Python across 4 services. 39 React components, 13 route handlers, 13
  shared schemas. This is verifiably not a concept deck.

  Gaps that cost points:

  - CRITICAL: Gemma version mismatch. The Kaggle writeup says "Gemma 4" throughout, but eval-baseline.md shows deployed
  endpoints are google/gemma3@gemma-3-4b-it and google/gemma3@gemma-3-27b-it. The actual harness.py MODEL_MAP references Gemma
   4, but the real inference was tested against Gemma 3 endpoints. If a judge checks the eval baseline or endpoint configs,
  this discrepancy would be damaging. You need to actually deploy and test against Gemma 4 models.
  - No Gemma 4-specific features used. Gemma 4 introduced native multimodal understanding (images), improved function calling,
   and new model sizes (E2B, E4B, 26B, 31B). The writeup claims "native function calling" but the code shows tool calling is
  spec-only with no execution logic. Multimodal input (worksheet photos) is mentioned in the architecture doc but not
  implemented. Using Gemma 4's actual new capabilities would significantly strengthen the technical story.
  - Mock vs real inference gap. The eval report shows 62/64 passing against real inference, but with Gemma 3. There's no
  validated baseline against Gemma 4. The two remaining failures (parse error, retrieval gap) are documented but unfixed.
  - Writeup metrics inconsistency. The Kaggle writeup says "~5,000 TypeScript lines" and "~750 Python lines" but actual counts
   are ~13,500 TS and ~1,900 Python. The writeup says "42 evals" but the real count is 64. These stale numbers suggest the
  writeup wasn't updated after later sprints.
  - Tool calling is speculative. The routing table marks routes as tool_call_capable: true but there is zero tool execution
  code. The writeup doesn't claim tool calling works, but the router design implies it. A judge reading the code would notice.
  - Retrieval service is a placeholder. services/retrieval/README.md says "Will use EmbeddingGemma for semantic search" but
  the directory is empty. Current retrieval is SQL-only. This is fine architecturally but the README shouldn't promise what
  doesn't exist.

  COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS: FUTURE OF EDUCATION TRACK

  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  The track description is: "Reimagine the learning journey by building multi-tool agents that adapt to the individual and
  empower the educator through seamless integration." PrairieClassroom OS hits every keyword: multi-tool (8 workflows), adapts
   to the individual (per-student differentiation), empowers the educator (teacher-facing, not student-facing), seamless
  integration (closed feedback loop). This is an unusually strong track fit.
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  What competing submissions will likely look like:
  - AI tutoring chatbots (student-facing, conversational) — common but generic
  - Automated grading/feedback tools — useful but narrow
  - Lesson plan generators — one-shot tools without memory
  - Accessibility assistants — important but typically single-purpose

  Your differentiation:
  - You serve the teacher, not the student — an underserved category
  - You have 8 integrated workflows, not one feature
  - You have classroom memory that compounds — the system gets better
  - You have safety constraints appropriate for K-6 — most submissions won't
  - You have a specific user story (Mrs. Okafor) — most submissions are generic

  Your vulnerability:
  - Submissions with polished videos and live demos will score higher even with simpler tech
  - Submissions that actually use Gemma 4's new capabilities (multimodal, native function calling) will be rewarded
  - Submissions with real user testimonials or field testing will win on impact
